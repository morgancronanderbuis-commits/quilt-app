# Quilting Techniques Reference Guide
*Notes from David Taylor, Quilter — Project Linus (Quilts for Kids in Need)*

---

## Borders

### Border Tips & Formulas

1. **Seam allowance**: Always subtract ½" for seam allowances before doing any border math.

2. **Spacer border width**: Measure the quilt and subtract ½" for seam allowances. Measure the pieced border and subtract ½". Subtract the quilt top measurement from the border measurement, then divide the difference by 2. This is the finished width of the spacer border. Add ½" when cutting strips.

3. **Grain**: Borders are more stable when cut on the **lengthwise grain** of the fabric.

4. **Diagonal measurement of a square**: Take the finished dimension and multiply by **1.414** (the hypotenuse). Always a weird number — round up to the nearest ⅛", then add 1.25".

5. **Setting triangle size**: Take the finished dimension of a block and multiply by 1.414. Round up to nearest ⅛". Add 1¼". Cut squares this size and cut into quarters with an X.

6. **Corner setting triangles**: Take the finished dimension of a block and divide by 1.414. Round up to nearest ⅛" and add 7/8" for seam allowances. Cut squares this size and cut in half diagonally.

### Border Basics (Plain Borders)

1. Inner borders may be cut on crosswise grain. Piece strips by cutting a 45° angle on ends and piecing end to end.
2. Outer borders should be cut on **lengthwise grain** for stability — quilts lay flatter.
3. Always measure quilt top before cutting border lengths. Measure opposite sides and through the center to find an average length.
4. Never start at one end and sew to the other. Find the center of the border and match to the center of the quilt top. Pin at center, each end, and every 6–8 inches. Then sew.
5. Press seams flat, then toward the last border added.

### Pieced Borders

1. If a pieced border is slightly long, pin border to quilt top (matching centers and ends) and sew with the **pieced border toward the feed dogs** — this will automatically ease the fullness.
2. **Spacer border formula**: Measure quilt top and pieced border; subtract ½" from each for seam allowances. Subtract quilt top measurement from pieced border measurement. Divide difference by 2 = finished spacer border dimension. Add ½" for seam allowances before cutting.

### Fabric Grain & Borders
- If the pattern isn't in the right direction, put crosswise on the bottom (of your machine) and lengthwise piece on top — to see what happens the wrong way.
- Tip: sewing crosswise on top, lengthwise on bottom → things will get wavy.
- **No crosswise on the border.**

## Triangles

### Half Square Triangles (HST)
- Add **7/8"** to the finished dimension of the desired triangle square.
- Cut squares this size and cut in half diagonally, OR draw a diagonal line corner to corner and stitch ¼" on either side of the drawn line. Cutting apart on the drawn line yields two triangle squares.
- Hypotenuse of the triangle needs to be on the **straight of grain** (not the bias of the grain — even if it wastes fabric). If everything is on bias, it will wave and stretch.
- "Triangle on a roll" makes it faster — sew a continuous line for half-square.
- Move needle to center, sew on dotted line. Stitch length 2 is good. Cut on the solid line.
- Press from the top of fabric.
- Match grain of squares: **crosswise on crosswise** and **lengthwise on lengthwise**.
- You could do 22" at a time. (She did 2 triangles and it was 16 squares!)
- **1/16th of an inch off is okay. ¼" is not.**

### Quarter Square Triangles (QST)
- Add **1¼"** to the finished dimension of the long side of the desired triangle.
- Cut squares this size and cut into quarters with an X.
- Take 2 squares and put together. Draw line on diagonal. Have needle at ¼" and sew ¼" on either side of diagonal. Cut in half, open, press to dark side. Place 2 squares together and draw diagonal line again. Do the same thing, sew ¼" on either side of diagonal line. You can spin the center — take out stitches leading up to the center on the seam allowance.

### Bias Squares
- **Bias means diagonal.** Normally we don't do bias, but sometimes we do.
- Many quilts contain squares made from two contrasting triangles. The short sides are on straight grain; the long side is on the bias. These are called **bias square units**.
- Using a bias strip piecing method, they can be easily sewn and cut in many sizes — especially useful for small bias squares where pressing after stitching usually distorts the shape.

**Bias Square Method:**
1. Cut two half-yard pieces of contrasting fabric and layer right sides together. Both will be cut at the same time.
2. Use the 45° marking on the cutting guide and make a bias cut.
3. Cut strips the same width as the bias squares you will be cutting. (Example: cut bias strips 2½" wide for 2½" unfinished bias squares = 2" finished.)
4. Sew strips together on the long bias edge using ¼" seam allowance. Press seams toward dark fabric. (For bias squares 1¼" or smaller, press seam open to evenly distribute bulk.)
5. Align 45° marking of the Bias Square™ with seamline. Cut first two sides of square. Measure from cut edge to opposite side of square and cut. Cut all four sides in the same manner.
6. Align 45° marking on Bias Square™ with seamline before cutting the next bias square.
7. Cut size for bias squares is given in all directions. Finished size will be ½" smaller.
8. Waste triangles will result after cutting. Several quilts incorporate these triangles into design units.

### Flying Geese (Quick Piecing)
*Uses 1 – 5¼" square & 4 – 2¾" squares. Yield: 4 Flying Geese Units.*

1. Draw a diagonal line on the wrong side of each of the 4 small squares. (Squares always have ¼" seam allowance when cutting.)
2. With right sides together, place 2 small squares in opposite corners of the large square — matching corners and center diagonal lines. Stitch ¼" on each side of drawn line.
3. Using a ruler and rotary cutter, cut on center lines. Press small triangles away from large triangle.
4. Place another marked square in the remaining corner of the large triangle. Stitch on seam lines. Cut and press. Yield 2 flying geese units.
5. Trim corners (ears) on each unit. Units should measure 2½" x 4½".
6. Repeat with remaining squares to yield 4 flying geese units (enough for 1 star block).

**Note**: If star points are two different colors, sew the small colored squares in pairs — first one color, then second color. Make a second set reversing color placement. Sort geese units so triangle color is the same in all for units.

**Tips (handwritten notes):**
- If you make something really small, press seams open; otherwise press to dark side.
- The tips may not go all the way up — that's okay. It's a part of the ¼" seam allowance.

## Batting & Finishing

### How to Pick Your Batting
- Most battings today: you can do **6–10 inches apart** for quilting.
- They carry Quilter's Dream, Hobbs — bamboo is good for pliability.
- Usually don't use polyester because the fibers are long and pill and beard (come through the fabric).
- Flannel quilt: maybe use — warm and natural to give more body and warmth.
- You can **baste** it to hold it first. Put a sheet beneath to protect the floor. Spray fabric, not batting. Spray in 20" segments. Spray and spray.
- You want a **little extra batting and backing** around.
- **Tie corners** with 6 strands of embroidery floss.
- Polyester doesn't breathe.
- Maybe don't put a wool one in the dryer.

### Free Motion Quilting (Stipple/Stitch Regulator)
- Stipple + stick on the fabric to try quilting.
- Drop the feed dogs! You may have a button or a lever.
- **Machine quilting foot** — you are the person moving the fabric.
- You can use **gardening gloves** — grippy is very helpful!
- A stitch regulator can be used.
- Get both threads on top of fabric to start.
- **Hand quilting**: start in center. Machine quilting not necessary.
- You can rent the long arm here.
- $1,000 foot for stitch regulator.

### General Tips
- Try taking a picture in B&W — this can show you the **contrast value**.
- On point — blocks □ are diagonal ◇.
- Everything on the edge of the quilt should be on straight grain.
- What if your blocks are supposed to be 12" but are 13"? Your average will work: (12.5 + 12 + 12.25 + 13) ÷ 5 = avg.
- As long as your variation is within an inch, you can place blocks in a way to get it to work. Don't put the 12" by the 13".
- If you have too small a block, you could try adding to it instead — make it bigger. You don't want to blunt your points.
- Borders: lengthwise is more stable. 1 YD: fold, cut into strips → stitch together.
- Helps to **fold first**, cut extra, check if straight, then fold and cut again.
- You help **square up your quilt** by using lengthwise.
- No harm in washing the quilts.
- **Always measure before you put borders on.**

## Binding

- Make it on a **45° angle**.
- Right sides together, sew ¼" in.
- She does **2⅜" inch binding**.
- **Mitered corner.**
- Trim before or after — personal preference. She does before trim.
- Moda makes marble binding.
- Most premade bindings are poly-cotton — you can feel the difference and it will affect how it lays together slightly.
- Could do a **decorative stitch** on top.
- Straight lines are much harder — never works.
- **Lighter weight in bobbin** — lighter # = lighter thread; larger # in bobbin.

## Pressing Tips

- **Press seams open** if making something really small; otherwise press to dark side.
- Press from the top of fabric.
- Steady Betty is good for ironing.
- **Stretch side to side** — lengthwise side to side is **vertical/horizontal** (not stretching).
- Fold seams towards dark fabric.
- Use a ruler adjacent. But 1 finger obj roller + rest of fingers on ruler anchors it.
- 45mm rotary cutter — can do 8 layers.
- Don't go more than 3x after 3x it means to be re-pressed.
- **InStyle prints** — if you can't see it from 3 feet away, it's meant to be there.
- Don't enough a press or handwash or use lingerie bag.
- **Crosswise grain (stretching) — horizontal.**
