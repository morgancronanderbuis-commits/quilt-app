Quilting Techniques Reference

Notes from David Taylor, Quilter — Project Linus (Quilts for Kids in Need)

Borders

Border Tips & Formulas

  1. Seam allowance: Always subtract ½" for seam allowances before doing any border math.
  2. Spacer border width: Measure the quilt and subtract ½" for seam allowances. Measure the pieced border and subtract ½". Subtract the quilt top measurement from the border measurement, then divide the difference by 2. This is the finished width of the spacer border. Add ½" when cutting strips.
  3. Grain: Borders are more stable when cut on the lengthwise grain of the fabric.
  4. Diagonal measurement of a square: Take the finished dimension and multiply by 1.414 (the hypotenuse). Always a weird number — round up to the nearest ⅛", then add 1.25".
  5. Setting triangle size: Take the finished dimension of a block and multiply by 1.414. Round up to nearest ⅛". Add 1¼". Cut squares this size and cut into quarters with an X.
  6. Corner setting triangles: Take the finished dimension of a block and divide by 1.414. Round up to nearest ⅛" and add 7/8" for seam allowances. Cut squares this size and cut in half diagonally.
Quick formula — diagonal of a square:
Finished size × 1.414 = diagonal length. Round up to nearest ⅛", then add 1¼".

Plain Borders

  1. Inner borders may be cut on crosswise grain. Piece strips by cutting a 45° angle on ends and piecing end to end.
  2. Outer borders should be cut on lengthwise grain for stability — quilts lay flatter.
  3. Always measure quilt top before cutting border lengths. Measure opposite sides and through the center to find an average length.
  4. Never start at one end and sew to the other. Find the center of the border and match to the center of the quilt top. Pin at center, each end, and every 6–8 inches. Then sew.
  5. Press seams flat, then toward the last border added.
Always measure before you put borders on. Your quilt top may have stretched unevenly — measuring and finding an average prevents wavy borders.

Pieced Borders

  1. If a pieced border is slightly long, pin border to quilt top (matching centers and ends) and sew with the pieced border toward the feed dogs — this will automatically ease the fullness.
  2. Spacer border formula: Measure quilt top and pieced border; subtract ½" from each for seam allowances. Subtract quilt top measurement from pieced border measurement. Divide difference by 2 = finished spacer border dimension. Add ½" for seam allowances before cutting.

Fabric Grain & Borders

No crosswise grain on borders. Sewing crosswise on top and lengthwise on the bottom causes waviness. Outer borders: always lengthwise grain.

Triangles & HSTs

Half Square Triangles (HST)

  • Add 7/8" to the finished dimension of the desired triangle square.
  • Cut squares this size and cut in half diagonally, or draw a diagonal line corner to corner and stitch ¼" on either side of the drawn line. Cutting apart on the drawn line yields two triangle squares.
  • Hypotenuse of the triangle needs to be on the straight of grain — not the bias. If everything is on bias, it will wave and stretch.
  • "Triangle on a roll" makes it faster — sew a continuous line for half-square.
  • Press from the top of fabric.
  • Match grain of squares: crosswise on crosswise and lengthwise on lengthwise.
HST cut size: Finished size + 7/8"
Example: Want 4½" finished HST? Cut squares at 5⅜".
  • 1/16th of an inch off is okay. ¼" is not.
  • Stitch length 2 is good for fine work.
  • You could do 22" at a time — two triangles yields 16 squares.

Quarter Square Triangles (QST)

  • Add 1¼" to the finished dimension of the long side of the desired triangle.
  • Cut squares this size and cut into quarters with an X.
  • Take 2 squares, put together. Draw line on diagonal. Have needle at ¼" and sew ¼" on either side of diagonal. Cut in half, open, press to dark side.
  • Place 2 squares together and draw diagonal line again. Sew ¼" on either side. You can spin the center — take out stitches leading up to the center on the seam allowance.
QST cut size: Finished size of long side + 1¼"
Yields 4 QST triangles per pair of squares.

Bias Squares

Bias means diagonal. Many quilts contain squares made from two contrasting triangles. Short sides are on straight grain; long side is on the bias. These are called bias square units.

  1. Cut two half-yard pieces of contrasting fabric and layer right sides together. Both will be cut at the same time.
  2. Use the 45° marking on the cutting guide and make a bias cut.
  3. Cut strips the same width as the bias squares you will be cutting. (Example: cut 2½" wide strips for 2½" unfinished = 2" finished.)
  4. Sew strips together on the long bias edge using ¼" seam allowance. Press seams toward dark fabric. (For bias squares 1¼" or smaller, press seam open to evenly distribute bulk.)
  5. Align 45° marking of the Bias Square™ with seamline. Cut first two sides of square. Measure from cut edge and cut. Cut all four sides in the same manner.
  6. Waste triangles will result after cutting — several quilts incorporate these into design units.

Flying Geese (Quick Piecing)

Uses 1 – 5¼" square & 4 – 2¾" squares. Yield: 4 Flying Geese Units.

  1. Draw a diagonal line on the wrong side of each of the 4 small squares.
  2. With right sides together, place 2 small squares in opposite corners of the large square — matching corners and center diagonal lines. Stitch ¼" on each side of drawn line.
  3. Using a ruler and rotary cutter, cut on center lines. Press small triangles away from large triangle.
  4. Place another marked square in the remaining corner of the large triangle. Stitch on seam lines. Cut and press. Yield 2 flying geese units.
  5. Trim corners (ears) on each unit. Units should measure 2½" × 4½".
  6. Repeat with remaining squares to yield 4 flying geese units (enough for 1 star block).
  • If star points are two different colors, sew small colored squares in pairs — first one color, then second. Make a second set reversing color placement.
  • The tips may not go all the way up — that's okay. It's part of the ¼" seam allowance.
  • If you make something really small, press seams open; otherwise press to dark side.

Batting & Finishing

Choosing Batting

  • Most battings today: you can do 6–10 inches apart for quilting.
  • Good brands: Quilter's Dream, Hobbs — bamboo is good for pliability.
  • Usually avoid polyester — fibers are long and pill and beard (come through the fabric). Polyester also doesn't breathe.
  • For a flannel quilt: warm and natural batting gives more body and warmth.
  • Wool batting: do not put in the dryer.

Basting

  • Put a sheet beneath to protect the floor. Spray fabric (not batting) in 20" segments.
  • You want a little extra batting and backing around all edges.
  • Tie corners with 6 strands of embroidery floss.
  • $50 tip: baste with 505 spray.

Free Motion Quilting

  • Drop the feed dogs — you may have a button or lever.
  • Use a machine quilting foot — you are the person moving the fabric.
  • Gardening gloves with grippy texture are very helpful for control.
  • A stitch regulator can be used for consistent stitch length.
  • Get both threads on top of fabric to start.
  • For hand quilting: start in the center. Machine quilting is not necessary.

General Tips

  • Try taking a picture in black & white — shows contrast value clearly.
  • Everything on the edge of the quilt should be on straight grain.
  • If your blocks are supposed to be 12" but come out slightly different, average them: (12.5 + 12 + 12.25 + 13) ÷ 4 = average. As long as variation is within an inch, you can arrange blocks to compensate.
  • If you have a block that's too small, try adding to it rather than discarding — don't blunt your points.
  • No harm in washing the quilts.

Binding

  • Cut binding strips on a 45° angle.
  • Right sides together, sew ¼" in.
  • Standard width: 2⅜" binding strips (some patterns use 2½").
  • Mitered corners.
  • Trim before or after — personal preference.
  • Moda makes marble binding.
  • Most premade bindings are poly-cotton — you can feel the difference and it affects how it lays.
  • Could do a decorative stitch on top.
  • Straight stitch lines are much harder — wavy stitching happens easily.
  • Lighter weight thread in bobbin — lighter # = lighter thread; larger # in bobbin.
Joining binding strips: cut a 45° angle on ends and sew end-to-end. Press seams open. Fold prepared strip in half wrong sides together and press before applying to quilt.

Reference sheet

Pressing Tips

  • Press seams open if making something really small; otherwise press to dark side.
  • Press from the top of fabric.
  • Fold seams towards dark fabric.
  • Stretch side to side — lengthwise side to side is vertical/horizontal (not stretching). Avoid stretching bias edges.
  • Use a ruler adjacent. One finger on the object + rest of fingers on ruler anchors it.
  • 45mm rotary cutter can do 8 layers.
  • Don't over-press — no more than 3x; after 3x it's meant to be there. Re-pressing can distort seams.
  • InStyle prints: if you can't see it from 3 feet away, it's meant to be there.
  • Crosswise grain = horizontal — and it stretches. Be careful pressing crosswise seams.
  • Steady Betty is good for ironing.

Miscellaneous Notes

  • EQ Printables works in an inkjet printer for designing blocks.
  • Chain piecing: sew pieces one after another in a chain — saves time and thread. Don't cut threads between pieces; clip them all at once afterward.
  • On point — blocks □ are set diagonal ◇.
  • Project Linus — Quilts for Kids in Need / Security Blanket program.

Charm Sunday

Moda Fabrics 64" × 87½" finished 12 charm packs HST blocks
Charm Pack

Seam allowance ¼" included in all measurements. WOF ≈ 42".

Charm Square Tally: Light 197 · Dark 249

Fabric Requirements

MaterialAmount
Charm packs12 packs
OR light fat quarters24
Dark fat quarters21
Binding⅝ yard
Backing6 yards

If using fat quarters, cut additional 35 light + 15 dark 5"×5" squares. From each fat quarter cut 3 – 5"×22" strips, then 12 – 5"×5" squares.

Cutting

Light Charm Squares:

  • From 4: cut 4 – 4½" × 4½" (#1 squares)
  • From 18: cut 18 – 4½" × 5" (#2 rectangles)
  • From 60: cut 120 – 2½" × 4½" rectangles
  • From 74: cut 296 – 2½" × 2½" squares

Dark Charm Squares:

  • From 130: cut 260 – 2½" × 5" rectangles
  • From 44: cut 88 – 2½" × 4½" rectangles
  • From 60: cut 240 – 2½" × 2½" squares

Binding: Cut 8 – 2½" × WOF strips.

Size reference: #1 = 4½"×4½" · #2 = 4½"×5" · #3 = 5"×5"

Block Construction

Step 1 — Half Square Triangle Blocks — Make 30

  1. Draw a diagonal line on the wrong side of 15 – 5"×5" light charm squares.
  2. Layer each with a dark 5"×5" square, right sides together.
  3. Sew ¼" on each side of the diagonal line.
  4. Cut apart on the diagonal line. Press open. Make 30 HST blocks. Finished: 4½" × 4½".

Step 2 — Block A — Make 65

  • Combine 4 – 2½" × 5" dark rectangles. Press in one direction. Finished: 5" × 8½".

Step 3 — Block B — Make 22

  1. Sew 1 – 2½" × 2½" light square diagonally to 1 – 2½" × 4½" dark rectangle. Press to corner.
  2. Sew another light square on the same rectangle. Press — one flying geese unit. Make 88.
  3. Sew together 2 flying geese units. Make 44.
  4. Combine 2 units to make 22 of Block B. Finished: 4½" × 8½".

Step 4 — Block C — Make 30

  1. Sew 1 – 2½" × 2½" dark square diagonally to 1 – 2½" × 4½" light rectangle. Press to corner.
  2. Sew another dark square on the same rectangle. Press — one flying geese unit. Make 120.
  3. Combine 4 flying geese units + 1 HST block + 4 – 2½" × 2½" light squares. Make 30 of Block C. Finished: 8½" × 8½".

Assembly

RowContentsQty
Row OneSew together 8 Block AsMake 2
Row TwoAlternate 6 – 4½"×4½" light squares (#1) and 5 Block BsMake 2
Row ThreeSew 2 Block Bs, 4 Block As, and 5 Block CsMake 6
Row FourAlternate 6 – 5"×5" light squares (#3) and 5 Block BsMake 5

Sew rows together. Layer, quilt, and bind. Enjoy!

Charm Monday

Moda Fabrics 58" × 83" finished 9 charm packs Flying Geese blocks
Charm Pack

Seam allowance ¼" included. WOF ≈ 42".

Charm Square Tally: Light 164 · Dark 186

Fabric Requirements

MaterialAmount
Charm packs9 packs
OR light fat quarters14
Dark fat quarters16
Binding⅝ yard
Backing5 yards

From each fat quarter cut 3 – 5"×22" strips, then 12 – 5"×5" squares.

Cutting

Light Charm Squares:

  • From 70: cut 140 – 2½" × 4½" rectangles
  • From 70: cut 280 – 2½" × 2½" squares

Dark Charm Squares:

  • From 70: cut 140 – 2½" × 4½" rectangles
  • From 116: make 116 "pieced" squares (see below)

Binding: Cut 8 – 2½" × WOF strips.

Making "Pieced" Charms

  1. Place two 5"×5" squares right sides together.
  2. Stitch a scant ⅛" seam on outside edges.
  3. Cut in half.
  4. Press open to make 2 "pieced" charms — 4½" × 5" with seams.

Make 116 pieced charms.

Block Construction

Block A — Make 58

  • Combine 2 "pieced" squares. Press in one direction. Finished: 5" × 8½".

Block B — Make 35

  1. Sew 1 – 2½"×2½" light square diagonally to 1 – 2½"×4½" dark rectangle. Press to corner — triangle unit.
  2. Sew another light square on the same rectangle. Press — one flying geese unit. Make 140.
  3. Add 1 – 2½"×4½" light rectangle to each flying geese unit. Make 140.
  4. Combine 4 of these units to make 35 of Block B. Finished: 8½" × 8½".

Assembly

  • Row One: Alternately sew together 4 Block As and 5 Block Bs. Press away from Block Bs. Make 7 of Row One.
  • Row Two: Alternately sew 5 Block As and 4 – 5"×5" light charm squares. Press away from the 5" squares. Make 6 of Row Two.

Join the rows. Layer, quilt as desired, and bind. Enjoy!

One Charm Pack Plus

Moda Fabrics — Prairie Bailey 36" × 36" finished 1 charm pack Simple bordered grid
Charm Pack

Seam allowance ¼" included. WOF ≈ 42".

Fabric Requirements

MaterialAmount
One Charm Pack40 – 5"×5" squares
Inner border⅓ yard
Final border & binding1 yard
Backing1¼ yards

Cutting

  • Inner Border: Cut 4 – 2" × 27½" strips
  • Final Border: Cut 4 – 3¾" × 27½" strips
  • Binding: Cut 4 – 2½" × WOF strips

Construction

Step 1 — Quilt Center: Sew together six rows of 5"×5" squares using all 40 squares from the charm pack. Press rows in opposite directions. Sew rows together.

Step 2 — Borders: Sew one 2" × 27½" inner border strip to one 3¾" × 27½" final border strip. Make 4 borders.

Step 3 — Assemble: Use the four 5"×5" corner squares, four borders, and the quilt center to assemble the quilt top.

Great beginner project — the quilt center is a straightforward 5×8 grid of charm squares. Use the planner's "Generate Layout" or "Optimize Colors" to arrange your fabrics.

William Morris Table Topper

Moda Fabrics — V&A Archives 18" × 36" finished 2 charm packs Hourglass / Pinwheel blocks
Charm Pack

Fabric Requirements

MaterialDetails
William Morris 2017 charm pack#7300PP
Moda Grunge charm pack (background/tan)#30150-162PP
Binding¼ yard William Morris 2017 (#7307-24)

Cutting

  • Using a ruler and rotary cutter, cut all 84 charm squares twice diagonally (quarter-square cuts) to make 336 quarter square triangles.
  • Cut 3 – 2½" × WOF strips for binding.

Construction

Each hourglass block requires 2 background (tan) and 2 print quarter square triangles.

Step 1 — Make Pairs: Stitch all triangles into pairs using 1 print and 1 background triangle. Always stitch pairs with the print on the right and the background on the left. Make 168 pairs. Press seams open.

Step 2 — Make Hourglass Blocks: Randomly select 2 pairs. Stitch together to make 1 hourglass block. Press seam open. Do not stitch matching prints together in the same block. Trim to 3½" × 3½". Make 84 blocks.

Step 3 — Assemble Table Topper:

  1. Sort blocks into sets of 4 with 1 matching print in each group of four.
  2. Position the four matching print triangles toward the center of the block to form a pinwheel. Press seams toward prints. Make 21 pinwheel blocks. You will use 18 of the 21 blocks.
  3. Arrange pinwheel blocks into 3 rows of 6. Stitch together. Press seams in alternate directions.
  4. Join rows. Topper should measure 18½" × 36½" with seams.

Finishing

  1. Join binding strips end to end with diagonal seams. Press open. Fold in half wrong sides together.
  2. Layer, quilt, and bind.

2nd Easiest Jelly Roll Quilt

50" × 64" finished 1 jelly roll (40 strips) 20 paired blocks
Jelly Roll

Materials

MaterialAmount
One jelly roll (or 40 assorted 2½" × 42" strips)—
Backing5/8 yard
Binding3¼ yards
Batting60" × 74"

Instructions

Use the Quilt Planner → Jelly Roll tab → 2nd Easiest preset to preview your strip pairings. Drag to rearrange which strips go in each numbered block before you start cutting.
  1. Pair jelly roll strips into twenty sets of two (blocks 1–20 in the planner).
  2. Sew strips together lengthwise.
  3. Press seams toward the darker of the two strips. Fold strip set in half lengthwise. Cut apart on fold. Turn one half of strip set 180° so that opposite colors are next to each other.
  4. Sew strip sets together again.
  5. Repeat until all strip sets are assembled.
  6. Join blocks together in rows following quilt layout. Make 20 blocks. Trim blocks to 10" in width.
  7. Cut backing fabric into two pieces 42" × 58½". Sew pieces together in a 58½" seam. Press seam open. (Seam will be crosswise on the back.)
  8. Cut 2½" bias strips from binding fabric.
  9. Layer backing, batting, and top. Quilt as desired.
  10. Sew binding strips together end to end in diagonal seams. Press seams open. Fold in half wrong sides together. Place right sides together with quilt top, sew ¼" seam. Bring folded edge to back and slipstitch in place.
  • Try to have seams press each other — iron towards the dark.
  • ⅜" rule: if you can't see it from 3 feet away, it's meant to be there.
  • 45mm rotary cutter can do 8 layers.
  • Don't use crosswise on the border — things will get wavy.
  • Chain piecing: sew pieces one after another without cutting thread between — saves time and thread.
  • Crosswise grain (stretching) = horizontal.

Easiest Ever Jelly Roll Throw

61" × 77" finished (twin) 1 jelly roll (40 strips) All strips end-to-end
Jelly Roll

Materials

MaterialAmount
One jelly roll—
Inner border¾ yard
Outer border & binding2 yards
Backing4 yards
BattingTwin size

Instructions

Use the Quilt Planner → Jelly Roll tab → Easiest Throw preset to arrange your strips in sewing order. The numbers 1–40 show the sequence they'll be sewn end-to-end. Drag strips to rearrange which fabric goes in each position.
  1. Sew each 2½" strip end to end with a diagonal seam. This makes one continuous strip approximately 1,600" long. (No need to worry about placement — there's no way to judge which fabrics will end up next to one another.)
  2. Trim seams to ¼" and press in one direction.
  3. Cut the first strip off approximately in the middle and discard the selvage off the 40th strip. Bring the two ends together — trim the loose end.
  4. Bring the two ends together — right sides together with no twists in the strips. Sew together the long sides together. Your strip is now 2 strips wide and about 800" long.
  5. Clip the folded end so that you can open the strips and press the seam in one direction.
  6. Again, bring the short ends together; sew the long sides right sides together; clip the fold and press. Each fold cuts the strip length in half and the seam becomes much easier to sew.
  7. Repeat step 6 until you have 32 strips in width.
  8. Square up the top.
  9. Cut six 2½" strips from the inner border fabric. Piece end to end. Find an average. Cut 2 strips for sides, 2 strips for top and bottom. Press seams toward outer border.
  10. Cut a 5⅜" border lengthwise from outer border fabric. Cut 2 strips for sides, 2 for top and bottom. Finished size: approximately 61" × 77".
  11. Layer backing, batting, and top. Quilt as desired.
  12. Bind using 2½" strips of outer border fabric.
  • Notes say: "Cut the best half — it's messy!"
  • There's no way to judge which fabrics end up next to each other — the randomness is part of the charm.
